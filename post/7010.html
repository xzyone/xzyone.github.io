Leisure Experience:

Three phases : anticipation, participation, reflection

Leisure

  • Those activities which people do in their free time, because they want to, for their own sake, for fun, entertainment, self-improvement, or for goals of their choosing, but not for material gain (Argyle, 1996, p. 3).
  • Through freedom of choice, an individual experiences self-determination (Dattilo & Kleiber, 1993).
  • Leisure is a broad concept encompassing play, recreation, games and sport (Rossman & Schlatter, 2003).

Meaning of Leisure

  1. Classical view of leisure (aristocratic)
  2. As a symbol of social class (race, occupation)
  3. As non-obligatory time (discretionary time)
  4. Activity (engage during free time)
  5. As a state of being marked by perceived freedom (anti-utilitarianism)
  6. As a spiritual expression (holistic state of being

Continuum Model of Leisure

  • John Neulinger’s theory of leisure is defined by a psychological state of mind containing two criteria for leisure: perceived freedom and intrinsic motivation.
  • This perceived freedom is a state of mind where one freely chooses to perform an activity.
  • Neulinger identified 6 states of mind: pure leisure, leisure-work, leisure-job, pure work, work-job, and pure job.

Games

  • Games are leisure experiences with formal rules that define the interactional contents, attempt to equalize the players, and define the role that skill and chance will play in determining the outcome (Rossman & Schlatter, 2003).
  • An organized activity; a more organized form of play intended for diversion, fun and enjoyment.
  • Playing a game is the voluntary attempt to overcome unnecessary obstacles. Games have rules.
  • Game rules indicate when and how it starts as well as when and how it comes to an end.
  • Game rules provide contingency plans for when the activity goes away.

Recreation

  • An activity in which people engage primarily for enjoyment and satisfaction (Dattilo & Murphy, 1999).
  • It is a form of human activity or experience carried on voluntarily in one’s free time, usually pleasurable, and without extrinsic or practical purpose.
  • It is more organized and occurs during leisure time. • Some form of organized activity intended for social ends (Kelly, 1990).

Meaning of Recreation

  1. Voluntary participation
  2. Wide range of activities and experiences (sports, games…)
  3. Intrinsic motivation (personalpleasure)
  4. Individual’s perception/feeling about the activity
  5. Social acceptability (as against drug use… )
  6. A social institution (collective behaviour, values….)

Play

  • Play is a spontaneous form of leisure.
  • Play is chosen freely.
  • It involves a lack of seriousness in which interaction is free flowing, and it progresses from place to place and takes on new forms as focus, needs and demands shift (Denzin, 1975).
  • It is normally non-purposeful.
  • We incline to trade work for play.

Sport

  • Sport is leisure that involves institutionalized competitive physical activity. Three key concepts define sport: physical exertion, rules and competition of physical skills (Rossman & Schlatter, 2003).
  • Organized competitions and usually governed by a set of regulations. Winning and losing is usually involved.
  • Sport (a more all-encompassing concept) vs. sports (a collection of separate activities).

Situated Activity System of Programme Design

  • All instances of leisure involve the interactions of one or more persons who are orienting their behavior toward themselves and other physical, social or symbolic objects (Denzin, 1975).
  • This co-orientation of selves occurs in a situated social system; that is, it occurs in a social occasion that has an identifiable set of elements that give it form and structure (Rossman & Schlatter, 2008).

Six Key Elements

Six Key Elements Details
Interacting people A successful event must be targeted for a specific group of individuals whom we can define with some precisions.
The physical setting A successful event must be targeted for a specific group of individuals whom we can define with some precisions.
Leisure objects Objects are anything that can be indicated, pointed out, or referred to (Blumer, 1969).
Human beings act toward objects based on the meanings that the objects have for them and their meaning is derived through interaction.
Rules The regulating effects that rules impose on a program cannot be underestimated.
Rules guide interactions in a program.
Unclear and constantly changing rules would cause anxiety and impinge perceived freedom for participants.
Relationships Participants in a program may have a preexisting relationship that binds them to each other.
To design a program properly, the programmer needs to know whether the participants have a relational history with each other. It would help understand how relationships contribute to or detract from client satisfaction.
Animation Animation deals with how a program is set into motion and how the action is sustained throughout the program.
It must be spontaneous and natural.
